# First Ten Pages Diagnostic — Block Calibration Sheet

This document calibrates the blocks most likely to drift into generic or abstract language.

It is an internal writing-and-review tool.
It does **not** add doctrine or categories.

---

## 1. Notable Strengths 1 — Reader pull

### What weak/generic writing looks like
- The opening has reader pull.
- The opening is engaging.
- The pages are compelling.
- The opening creates enough uncertainty and pressure.

### What acceptable concrete page behavior looks like
- the opening replaces one active problem with the next
- scenes end by pushing the read into another beat
- the pages withhold resolution instead of settling early
- early questions are generated through live action, not just atmosphere

### Strong example phrasings
1. **The opening has reader pull because the first pages keep replacing one live problem with the next rather than settling the situation.**
2. **The read keeps moving because each early beat ends by pushing the reader into another question or pressure point.**
3. **The opening sustains reader pull by withholding resolution while still changing the situation scene to scene.**

### Weak example phrasings to avoid
1. The opening is engaging.
2. The pages are compelling.
3. The opening creates interest.

### Rule
Do not stop at “reader pull.” Name **how** the pages create it.

---

## 2. Notable Strengths 2 — Protagonist strength

### What weak/generic writing looks like
- The protagonist has a strong presence.
- The lead is compelling.
- The protagonist gives the opening a center.
- The lead works well.

### What acceptable concrete page behavior looks like
- the protagonist arrives under visible pressure
- the early scenes organize themselves around the protagonist’s problem
- the first appearance is active and specific
- the opening keeps returning to the protagonist as the active center of attention

### Strong example phrasings
1. **The protagonist anchors the opening because the first appearance is active, specific, and already under pressure.**
2. **The early pages organize themselves around the protagonist’s problem rather than drifting away from it.**
3. **The opening locks onto the protagonist quickly and stays there through the early scene sequence.**

### Weak example phrasings to avoid
1. The protagonist has a strong presence.
2. The lead is compelling.
3. The opening gives the story a center.

### Rule
Use **anchors the opening** as the default protagonist-strength phrasing unless a more concrete page-based version is clearly stronger.

---

## 3. Notable Strengths 3 — World / setting strength

### What weak/generic writing looks like
- The setting helps the read.
- The world is vivid.
- The world has texture.
- The environment is effective.

### What acceptable concrete page behavior looks like
- the opening gives place and condition cues early
- the setting shapes scene pressure or mood
- the environment is shown through working conditions or social conditions
- the reader can orient to the world without guessing

### Strong example phrasings
1. **The world is doing useful work because the opening shows the setting through working conditions and social pressure rather than generic description.**
2. **The setting helps the read because the pages make the environment legible through place and condition cues early.**
3. **The opening feels placed rather than generic because the setting is shaping the scene, not sitting behind it.**

### Weak example phrasings to avoid
1. The world is immersive.
2. The setting is vivid.
3. The environment has texture.

### Rule
Do not praise the world without naming what on the page makes it legible.

## 4. Tone / mood / genre

### What weak/generic writing looks like
- The tone is steady.
- The mood is clear.
- The genre works.
- The opening knows what it is.

### What acceptable concrete page behavior looks like
- dialogue, environment, and scene pressure point in the same direction
- the reader is not asked to recalibrate how to read the material
- scenes avoid mixed tonal signals
- comic, dramatic, romantic, or suspense elements are framed consistently

### Strong example phrasings
1. **The dialogue, scene pressure, and environment are all pushing in the same direction, which keeps the opening tonally legible.**
2. **The opening does not ask the reader to keep recalibrating how to read the material from scene to scene.**
3. **What keeps this category strong is that the pages frame conflict, setting, and dialogue through the same tonal lens rather than splitting into competing versions of themselves.**

### Weak example phrasings to avoid
1. The tone is strong.
2. The mood works.
3. The genre is effective.

### Rule
Name at least two tonal carriers on the page and say what they do for the read.

---

## 5. World / setting

### What weak/generic writing looks like
- The world is readable.
- The setting is clear.
- The opening feels placed.
- The dramatic environment is legible.

### What acceptable concrete page behavior looks like
- the opening gives specific place or condition cues
- the environment around the protagonist is shown through scene detail
- the setting affects mood, pressure, or clarity
- the reader can understand the world without guessing at basic conditions

### Strong example phrasings
1. **The opening makes the world legible through concrete place and condition cues rather than generic labels alone.**
2. **The pages show what the environment feels like around the protagonist, not just where the story is set.**
3. **The setting is doing useful work because it is shaping the scene rather than sitting behind it as background.**

### Weak example phrasings to avoid
1. The world is immersive.
2. The setting has texture.
3. The environment is vivid.

### Rule
Name at least one concrete world cue and say how it affects the read.

---

## 6. Character introduction

### What weak/generic writing looks like
- The characters are distinct.
- The protagonist is memorable.
- The cast is easy to follow.
- The lead is introduced well.

### What acceptable concrete page behavior looks like
- characters arrive through behavior, pressure, or attitude
- each important character gets a specific readable signature
- the protagonist’s first appearance shows how they function under strain
- supporting characters are differentiated quickly through speech or conduct

### Strong example phrasings
1. **The protagonist is introduced through behavior and pressure, which makes the character readable immediately.**
2. **The opening gives the reader something specific to hold onto with each major character.**
3. **The pages differentiate the main players through action, speech, and pressure rather than labels alone.**

### Weak example phrasings to avoid
1. The characters are memorable.
2. The protagonist is distinct.
3. The cast comes through strongly.

### Rule
Do not say a character is strong or memorable unless you name what the pages make them do.

---

## 7. Empathy / reader care

### What weak/generic writing looks like
- The reader can care about the protagonist.
- There is enough empathy here.
- The character is sympathetic.
- The lead is relatable.

### What acceptable concrete page behavior looks like
- the protagonist is under visible strain
- the pages show embarrassment, isolation, vulnerability, or failure
- the protagonist is trying to hold onto something or survive something
- the reader gets a human access point through behavior rather than summary

### Strong example phrasings
1. **The pages create investment by putting the protagonist under visible strain early.**
2. **The opening shows the character failing, avoiding, or absorbing pressure in a way that gives the reader a human entry point.**
3. **The empathy is not abstract; the pages keep showing the cost of the protagonist’s situation in behavior and reaction.**

### Weak example phrasings to avoid
1. The character is relatable.
2. The lead is sympathetic.
3. The reader can connect with the protagonist.

### Rule
Name the visible strain or vulnerability on the page. Do not substitute emotional summary for page behavior.

---

## 8. Goals / motivations / obstacles / stakes

### What weak/generic writing looks like
- The stakes are there.
- There is pressure.
- Something matters.
- The motivation works.
- The line of pursuit is unclear.
- The objective is weak.

### What acceptable concrete page behavior looks like
- the pages show what the protagonist is trying to do right now
- the pages show what is blocking that effort
- the pages show what it will cost if the protagonist fails
- the opening shows the protagonist in a problem but not yet a clear next move
- what is blocking the protagonist is more visible than what the protagonist is trying to do next

### Strong example phrasings
1. **The opening makes the protagonist’s problem clear, but by page 10 it is still not clear enough what the protagonist is trying to do next.**
2. **What is blocking the protagonist is clearer than what the protagonist is trying to do in response.**
3. **The pages show that failure will cost the protagonist something, but they still do not define the protagonist’s next move clearly enough.**
4. **The opening gives the reader a problem to register, but not yet a clear enough action to follow.**
5. **This category is mixed: the pressure is visible on the page, but the protagonist’s response is still too loose to give the opening a strong forward line.**

### Weak example phrasings to avoid
1. The stakes are there, but they need to be higher.
2. There is pressure in the opening.
3. The motivation works, but the conflict could be stronger.
4. The line of pursuit is still unclear.
5. Something important is happening, but the goals need more definition.

### Rule
A block in this category must answer at least one of these:
- what is the protagonist trying to do right now?
- what is getting in the way?
- what will it cost if the protagonist fails?

## 9. Dialogue distinctness and control

### What weak/generic writing looks like
- The dialogue is strong.
- The dialogue works.
- The voices are distinct.
- The dialogue is sharp.

### What acceptable concrete page behavior looks like
- speakers are differentiated clearly
- dialogue is doing scene work, not just carrying explanation
- voices reveal pressure, attitude, or relationship dynamics
- exchanges change the present scene instead of standing still

### Strong example phrasings
1. **The dialogue differentiates people cleanly on the page and carries real scene pressure.**
2. **The voices are distinct, but some exchanges begin carrying explanation more than movement.**
3. **The dialogue works best when it sharpens conflict or character, and weakens when it starts delivering context too visibly.**

### Weak example phrasings to avoid
1. The dialogue is sharp.
2. The voices are strong.
3. The dialogue works well.

### Rule
Always say what the dialogue is doing besides sounding good.

---

## 10. Visible exposition

### What weak/generic writing looks like
- The exposition is heavy.
- There is too much backstory.
- The pages explain too much.
- The setup is exposition-heavy.

### What acceptable concrete page behavior looks like
- a scene pauses to explain conditions instead of moving forward
- dialogue carries relationship or world information too directly
- explanation sits more visibly on the page than the surrounding action
- backstory begins competing with present-scene movement

### Strong example phrasings
1. **The pages have a lot of setup to carry, but much of it is embedded in conflict rather than dropped inertly.**
2. **Exposition becomes more visible when the scene stops the present action long enough to explain relationship or world conditions directly.**
3. **The issue is not information itself; it is that explanation begins to sit too visibly on the dialogue.**

### Weak example phrasings to avoid
1. There is too much exposition.
2. The backstory is heavy.
3. The setup is exposition-heavy.

### Rule
Do not attack information in the abstract. Name where explanation becomes visible and what it displaces.

---

## 11. Scene utility / earning its space

### What weak/generic writing looks like
- Some scenes could be cut.
- The pacing is slow.
- The opening feels setup-heavy.
- Scenes do not move enough.

### What acceptable concrete page behavior looks like
- scenes repeat the same function
- scenes clarify context without changing direction
- stronger scenes add pressure or movement
- weaker scenes add setup without altering the active line
- the opening earns attention more easily than direction

### Strong example phrasings
1. **The stronger scenes add pressure or direction; the weaker ones repeat context the reader already understands.**
2. **The opening earns attention easily; it earns direction less consistently.**
3. **Several scenes are lively and readable, but they are not all sharpening the same forward line.**

### Weak example phrasings to avoid
1. The pacing is slow.
2. Some scenes could be cut.
3. The opening is too setup-heavy.

### Rule
Do not use pacing language as a substitute for scene-function analysis.

---

## 12. Secondary Findings — distinct take on familiar material

### What weak/generic writing looks like
- The script has a unique voice.
- The material feels fresh.
- The opening is distinctive.
- The pages are specific.
- The story stands out.

### What acceptable concrete page behavior looks like
- the opening stages familiar pressures in a non-generic environment
- the protagonist’s immediate problem feels specific on the page
- the setup is more particular than the story direction
- the opening’s specificity is visible in character, world, or pressure even if the larger line is less defined

### Strong example phrasings
1. **What feels least generic here is the way the opening presents the protagonist’s immediate problem and surroundings.**
2. **The pages are more specific in the pressure around the protagonist than in the story direction.**
3. **The opening gets its distinctiveness from the protagonist’s situation and the world around it.**

### Weak example phrasings to avoid
1. The script has a unique voice.
2. The material feels fresh.
3. The opening is distinctive.

### Rule
This block must identify:
- what specifically feels non-generic on the page
- and what is still less specific by comparison

---

## 13. Final reminder

Every block should be able to survive this test:

- Does it make a judgment?
- Does it name page behavior?
- Does it explain why that matters to the read?
- Could it be pasted into another report unchanged?

If the answer to the last question is yes, it is not ready.
