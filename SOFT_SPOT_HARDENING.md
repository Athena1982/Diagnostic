# Soft-Spot Hardening Sheet  
**First Ten Pages Diagnostic**

---

## 1. Tone / mood / genre

### What still tends to sound soft or vague
- “The tone is steady.”
- “The mood is clear.”
- “The pages establish their dramatic identity.”
- “The genre reads clearly.”
- “The opening commits to a consistent dramatic mode.”

### Why it remains vulnerable even after the current refinements
This category naturally invites summary language. It is easy to say the tonal signal is consistent without showing what on the page is making it consistent. That makes it one of the easiest categories to fake with polished prose.

### What stronger page-based phrasing should replace it
Replace summary-level tone language with page-based observations about:
- how **dialogue behaves**
- how **scene pressure is framed**
- how **the environment supports the same dramatic mode**
- whether scenes **change the reader’s tonal interpretation** or keep it stable

### 5 stronger example phrasings
1. **The tone holds because the dialogue, scene pressure, and environment are all pushing the material in the same direction.**
2. **The opening does not ask the reader to keep recalibrating how to read the material; each early scene reinforces the same dramatic mode.**
3. **What keeps this category strong is that the opening frames conflict, setting, and dialogue through the same tonal lens instead of splitting into competing versions of itself.**
4. **The pages stay tonally legible because the opening’s pressure never turns comic, ironic, or heightened in ways that undercut the dramatic mood already established.**
5. **The opening is steadier in how it frames tension than in where it sends the story, which keeps the tone readable even when page-10 direction is weaker.**

### 5 soft/vague phrasings to avoid
1. “The tone is effective.”
2. “The mood is compelling.”
3. “The genre is strong.”
4. “The pages know what they are.”
5. “The dramatic identity is clear.”

### 3 hardening rules
1. **Name at least two tonal carriers on the page**  
   Example: dialogue pressure + scene framing; environment + conflict treatment.
2. **State whether the opening avoids mixed signals**  
   A tone block should say whether the reader is being asked to reinterpret the material scene to scene.
3. **Tie tone to reader effect, not just category success**  
   Say what tonal consistency does for the read: stabilizes it, keeps it legible, prevents tonal drift.

### Short enforcement rule
Reject the block if it says the tone/mood/genre is “clear” or “working” without naming what on the page is creating that consistency.

## 2. World / setting

### What still tends to sound soft or vague
- “The world is readable.”
- “The setting is clear.”
- “The opening feels placed.”
- “The environment supports the read.”
- “The dramatic environment is legible.”

### Why it remains vulnerable even after the current refinements
This category often collapses into tasteful atmosphere language. It is easy to say the setting “works” without showing how the opening orients the reader or what details are doing the work.

### What stronger page-based phrasing should replace it
Replace soft world language with observations about:
- the **specific place or condition cues** the opening gives early
- how the pages show the **environment around the protagonist**
- whether the reader can understand the world without guessing at basic conditions
- whether the setting is affecting mood, pressure, or readability

### 5 stronger example phrasings
1. **The setting is clear because the opening gives the reader place and condition cues early instead of relying on location labels alone.**
2. **The world helps the read because the pages show what the environment feels like around the protagonist, not just where the story is set.**
3. **The opening orients the reader through concrete scene detail, so the dramatic environment is legible without extra explanation.**
4. **What works here is that the setting is shown through usable details that shape the scene, rather than sitting behind it as generic background.**
5. **The world is easier to understand than the story direction because the opening makes the protagonist’s surroundings legible almost immediately.**

### 5 soft/vague phrasings to avoid
1. “The world is immersive.”
2. “The setting has texture.”
3. “The environment is vivid.”
4. “The world-building works.”
5. “The pages feel atmospheric.”

### 3 hardening rules
1. **Name at least one concrete world cue**  
   Example: condition, place detail, environmental pressure, visible surrounding circumstances.
2. **Say how the setting affects the read**  
   Orientation, mood, pressure, or clarity.
3. **Do not let atmosphere stand in for legibility**  
   If the block only says the setting feels vivid, it is still too soft.

### Short enforcement rule
Reject the block if it praises the setting without naming what specific environmental detail or condition makes the world legible on the page.

## 3. Shared anti-templating alternatives

### What still tends to sound soft or vague
Even improved alternatives can become soft if repeated too often, especially:
- “This category is working.”
- “The opening is on firmer ground here.”
- “This element is helping carry the read.”
- “The pages are stronger in X than in Y.”
- “The opening creates enough uncertainty and pressure…”

### Why it remains vulnerable even after the current refinements
These phrases are useful, but because they are versatile, they can become default language. Once that happens, they stop sounding diagnostic and start sounding like polished stock prose.

### What stronger page-based phrasing should replace it
Replace reusable evaluative phrases with:
- **what the pages are doing**
- **what the reader is being asked to hold**
- **what is changing or not changing from scene to scene**
- **what specific page behavior is helping or weakening the read**

### 5 stronger example phrasings
1. **The early scenes keep ending by pushing the reader into the next beat, which is what sustains the opening’s pull.**
2. **This part of the opening is steadier because the pages are doing one job clearly instead of splitting between competing functions.**
3. **The read stays intact here because the scene is adding new pressure rather than restating the problem the reader already understands.**
4. **What helps this block is that the pages make X legible through Y, instead of asking the reader to infer too much.**
5. **This section works because the opening is not merely presenting X; it is putting X into motion on the page.**

### 5 soft/vague phrasings to avoid
1. “This category is working.”
2. “The opening is solid here.”
3. “This element supports the read.”
4. “The pages are effective in this area.”
5. “This part of the script is strong.”

### 3 hardening rules
1. **Do not end the sentence at evaluation**  
   “working,” “strong,” or “solid” must be followed by page behavior.
2. **Replace general praise with a mechanism**  
   Say how the opening creates the result.
3. **Avoid reusable praise shells**  
   If the sentence could be pasted into multiple categories with only one noun changed, it is too generic.

### Short enforcement rule
Reject the block if its main sentence could survive unchanged in another category or another report without sounding obviously out of place.

## Anti-template rotation rules

### Which variation phrases are at risk of becoming the new template
- “This category is working.”
- “The opening is on firmer ground here.”
- “The opening creates enough uncertainty and pressure…”
- “The pages are stronger in X than in Y.”
- “What works here is…”

### How often they should be rotated
- Do not use the same evaluative opening more than **once per report** unless strongly justified.
- Do not use the same comparison pattern more than **once in adjacent blocks**.
- Do not reuse the same sentence frame across multiple reports without alternating the lead structure.

### What kind of sentence openings should be alternated
Rotate among:
1. **Judgment-first**  
   “This is the main weak point…”
2. **Page-behavior-first**  
   “The opening keeps returning to…”
3. **Reader-effect-first**  
   “The reader is left with…”
4. **Comparison-led**  
   “The opening is stronger in X than in Y.”
5. **Cause-led**  
   “This holds because…”
6. **Contrast-led**  
   “What works here is X; what remains weaker is Y.”

### Short rule for preventing polished repetition across multiple reports
Repeat the diagnostic lens, not the sentence shell. If a phrase starts sounding smooth but reusable, replace it with a sentence that names what the pages are actually doing in this specific report.
