# Category Refinement Sheet  
**First Ten Pages Diagnostic**

---

## 1. Tone / mood / genre

### What recurring weak/generic phrasing currently looks like
- “This category is working.”
- “The tone is steady.”
- “The dramatic mood is clear.”
- “The pages establish their identity clearly.”
- “The genre reads clearly on the page.”

### Why that phrasing feels templated or abstract
These lines make a judgment, but they do not show what on the page is creating it. They sound like category labels, not observations. They can be reused too easily across unrelated reports without sounding obviously wrong.

### What concrete page behavior the block should name instead
This block should name:
- whether **scene pressure**, **dialogue style**, and **environmental framing** are all pointing toward the same dramatic mode
- whether the opening avoids **mixed tonal signals**
- whether the reader is being asked to reinterpret the material from scene to scene, or not
- whether the opening is clearer about mood than about story direction, if that comparison matters

### 5 stronger example phrasings
1. **The tone holds because the opening’s scene pressure, dialogue, and environment are all pointing in the same dramatic direction.**
2. **The pages do not ask the reader to keep reinterpreting the material; the opening commits to a consistent dramatic mood early.**
3. **The genre reads clearly because the opening frames conflict, dialogue, and setting through the same tonal lens instead of mixing signals.**
4. **What helps here is tonal consistency: the scenes are not pulling the material toward competing versions of itself.**
5. **The opening is clearer about how it wants to be read than it is about where the story is going, which keeps this category on firm ground.**

### 5 weak phrasings to avoid
1. “The tone is strong.”
2. “The mood works.”
3. “The pages know what they are.”
4. “The genre is effective.”
5. “This category is solid.”

### 3 phrase-variation patterns
1. **Cause-led:**  
   “This holds because the opening’s X, Y, and Z are all working in the same direction.”
2. **Reader-led:**  
   “The reader is not being asked to reinterpret the material from scene to scene.”
3. **Comparison-led:**  
   “The opening is stronger in tonal control than in story direction.”

### Short rule for when the category should be brief vs more developed
- **Brief** when tone/genre clarity is clearly working and not a pressure point in the report.
- **More developed** when the opening’s mood is clear but its story direction is not, or when tonal consistency is doing important work in stabilizing the read.

### Short rule for how to keep the category premium, page-based, and non-generic
Do not say the tone “works” unless you name what on the page is holding it together: scene pressure, dialogue treatment, environment, or absence of mixed signals.

## 2. World / setting

### What recurring weak/generic phrasing currently looks like
- “The world is readable.”
- “The setting is clear on the page.”
- “The world supports the opening.”
- “The environment is effective.”
- “The setting is well established.”

### Why that phrasing feels templated or abstract
These lines sound polished, but they do not identify how the opening is making the world legible. They often describe the result without naming the means.

### What concrete page behavior the block should name instead
This block should name:
- what **specific place or condition cues** the opening gives early
- how the setting affects **mood, pressure, or legibility**
- whether the world is being shown through **scene detail** rather than generic labeling
- whether the reader can understand the dramatic environment without guessing

### 5 stronger example phrasings
1. **The setting is clear because the opening gives the reader specific place and condition cues early instead of relying on generic location labels alone.**
2. **The world helps the read because the pages show the conditions around the protagonist, not just where the story takes place.**
3. **The opening feels placed rather than generic because scene detail is doing real orienting work on the page.**
4. **The reader can understand the dramatic environment without guessing, because the setting is shown through usable details rather than left as background.**
5. **What works here is not just that the story has a location; it is that the opening makes the world’s conditions legible quickly enough to support the read.**

### 5 weak phrasings to avoid
1. “The world is immersive.”
2. “The setting has texture.”
3. “The environment is vivid.”
4. “The world-building works.”
5. “The pages feel atmospheric.”

### 3 phrase-variation patterns
1. **Orientation-led:**  
   “The opening orients the reader through X rather than leaving the environment generic.”
2. **Condition-led:**  
   “What makes the world legible here is that the pages show X condition around the protagonist early.”
3. **Function-led:**  
   “The setting is doing more than providing background; it is shaping X in the scene.”

### Short rule for when the category should be brief vs more developed
- **Brief** when the setting is simply clear and not materially affecting the main diagnosis.
- **More developed** when the world is helping stabilize the read, when the setting carries mood or pressure, or when the environment is clearer than the story direction.

### Short rule for how to keep the category premium, page-based, and non-generic
State what the pages show about the world—place, condition, atmosphere, or rules—not just that the world is “clear” or “effective.”

## 3. Secondary Findings: Have a distinct take on familiar material

### What recurring weak/generic phrasing currently looks like
- “The script has a distinct take.”
- “The material feels specific.”
- “This does not feel generic.”
- “The script has a unique voice.”
- “The opening feels fresh.”

### Why that phrasing feels templated or abstract
These lines praise distinctiveness without naming where it is visible. They sound elevated, but they often lack comparison and page behavior.

### What concrete page behavior the block should name instead
This block should name:
- what specifically feels **non-generic on the page**
- whether that specificity is showing up in:
  - the protagonist’s immediate problem
  - the setting or pressure environment
  - the way scenes frame the central situation
- what is still **less specific by comparison**
- whether the opening is more distinct in **setup** than in **story direction**

### 5 stronger example phrasings
1. **What feels least generic here is the way the opening presents the protagonist’s immediate problem and surroundings. What is still less defined is where the story is going, which keeps that specificity from carrying through the full opening.**
2. **The pages are more specific in the pressure around the protagonist than in the story direction. The setup feels particular on the page, but the action it is meant to launch is still less clearly shaped.**
3. **The opening gets its distinctiveness from the protagonist’s situation and the world around it. What remains more familiar is the larger movement of the story, which is still not defined sharply enough by page 10.**
4. **The script feels most specific in how it stages the protagonist’s immediate pressure. What is still less distinct is the direction that pressure is meant to push the story toward.**
5. **The opening does not feel generic in its character-and-world setup. What still needs more definition is the story direction, so that the specificity already on the page carries into the larger read.**

### 5 weak phrasings to avoid
1. “The script has a unique voice.”
2. “This feels fresh.”
3. “The material stands out.”
4. “The opening is distinctive.”
5. “The story itself needs to stand out more.”

### 3 phrase-variation patterns
1. **Comparison-led:**  
   “The pages are more specific in X than in Y.”
2. **Non-generic anchor:**  
   “What feels least generic here is X; what is still less defined is Y.”
3. **Carry-through pattern:**  
   “The opening has specificity in X, but that specificity has not yet carried through into Y.”

### Short rule for when the category should be brief vs more developed
- **Brief** when the finding is secondary and only lightly useful.
- **More developed** when the opening has a meaningful non-generic quality that helps the read, especially if that specificity contrasts with a weaker story direction.

### Short rule for how to keep the category premium, page-based, and non-generic
Do not praise “distinctiveness” in the abstract. Name what specifically feels particular on the page, then name what remains less specific by comparison.

## Shared anti-templating fixes

### 5 overused report phrases to reduce, with better alternatives

1. **Overused:** “This category is working.”  
   **Better alternatives:**  
   - “This is one of the steadier parts of the opening.”  
   - “The opening is on firmer ground here.”  
   - “This element is helping carry the read.”

2. **Overused:** “The opening creates enough uncertainty and pressure…”  
   **Better alternatives:**  
   - “The first pages keep withholding resolution, which helps the read keep moving.”  
   - “The opening keeps the protagonist under active strain, which sustains reader pull.”  
   - “The scenes end by pushing the read forward rather than settling the situation.”

3. **Overused:** “The world is readable / clear on the page.”  
   **Better alternatives:**  
   - “The opening gives the reader concrete place and condition cues early.”  
   - “The setting is shown through usable details rather than generic labels.”  
   - “The dramatic environment is legible without forcing the reader to guess at it.”

4. **Overused:** “The pages establish their dramatic identity clearly.”  
   **Better alternatives:**  
   - “The opening’s scene pressure, dialogue, and environment are all pointing in the same direction.”  
   - “The pages do not keep shifting how the material wants to be read.”  
   - “The opening commits to a consistent dramatic mode early.”

5. **Overused:** “The opening gives the reader…”  
   **Better alternatives:**  
   - “The first pages make X legible through Y.”  
   - “What the opening establishes early is X, mainly through Y.”  
   - “The pages put X on the page quickly enough to support the read.”

### Short rule for varying sentence openings across reports
Do not begin consecutive blocks with the same formula. Rotate among:
- **judgment-first** openings  
  “This is the main weak point…”
- **page-behavior-first** openings  
  “The opening keeps returning to…”
- **reader-effect-first** openings  
  “The reader is left with…”
- **comparison-led** openings  
  “The opening is stronger in X than in Y.”
- **contrast-led** openings  
  “What works here is X; what remains weaker is Y.”

Repeat the framework, not the sentence shape.
