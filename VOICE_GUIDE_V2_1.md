# First Ten Pages Diagnostic — Voice Guide v2.1

## 1. Purpose

This guide defines the working voice for the **First Ten Pages Diagnostic**.

It exists to make reports sound:
- premium
- specific
- page-based
- non-generic
- non-workshop-like
- recognizably diagnostic rather than like screenplay coverage

This is an internal guide. It governs:
- report writing
- prompt design
- quality control
- audit review

It does **not** add new doctrine or categories.

---

## 2. Core voice standard

The report voice should be:

- **decisive**
- **restrained**
- **observant**
- **diagnostic**
- **writer-facing**
- **specific to the actual pages**
- **premium without sounding literary**
- **clear without sounding instructional**

The report should sound like:
- a sharp first-read diagnosis
- a structured professional assessment
- a system that sees what the pages are doing

The report should **not** sound like:
- screenplay coverage
- development notes
- a workshop critique
- a pep talk
- a blog post
- a teacher marking up student work

---

## 3. Core signature terms vs supporting language

### Core signature terms
These are the strongest recurring system phrases and should anchor the report language:

- **reader pull**
- **page-10 direction**
- **story direction**
- **visible exposition**
- **earned scene / earning its space**
- **anchors the opening**

These terms should recur across the system, but not mechanically in every paragraph.

### Supporting language
Use as needed for variation and precision:

- clear central presence
- forward movement
- drag in the read
- active pressure point
- setup-heavy
- distributed direction
- still consolidating
- strong focal line
- live problem
- explanatory weight
- present-scene movement
- active pressure
- dominant line of action
- directional promise
- the opening is stronger in X than in Y

Supporting language should serve the page-based observation. It should not become ornamental shorthand.

---

## 4. Distinction between page-10 direction and story direction

### Page-10 direction
This is the threshold judgment:
- by page 10, does the reader have a sufficiently clear sense of what the script is about and what direction it is beginning to take?

It uses the calibrated three-way distinction:
- **clear and consolidated**
- **clear but distributed / still consolidating**
- **insufficiently clear**

### Story direction
This is broader explanatory language used inside the report body:
- what line the opening is beginning to privilege
- what pressure is shaping the story
- what direction is emerging from the setup

Do not collapse these into one interchangeable term.

---

## 5. Preferred sentence patterns

These are preferred sentence patterns, not mandatory templates. Rotate them.

### Top-Line Summary
Use one central contrast only.

Preferred patterns:
- **These pages have X, but they do not yet have enough Y.**
- **The opening gives the reader X, but by page 10 it is still spending more energy on Y than on Z.**
- **The opening is strong in X and Y; the main limiting issue is Z.**
- **The direction is present, but still distributed across several active pressures.**

Use sparingly:
- **This is not an absence of X so much as a case of Y.**
- **The opening is not unclear so much as layered.**

These can be useful, but they can also start to sound system-heavy if repeated too often.

### Strengths
Preferred patterns:
- **The opening has reader pull because…**
- **The protagonist anchors the opening because…**
- **The world is doing useful work because…**
- **This is one of the steadier parts of the opening because…**
- **The opening is on firmer ground here because…**

### Weaknesses
Preferred patterns:
- **This is the main weak point in the opening.**
- **By page 10, the reader still does not know clearly enough…**
- **The opening keeps X without yet Y.**
- **What is visible is X; what is still less clear is Y.**
- **The pages add pressure faster than they consolidate direction.**

Use sparingly:
- **The issue is not X so much as Y.**
- **The opening is not lacking X so much as struggling with Y.**

### Evidence Notes
Preferred patterns:
- **Pages X–Y keep doing…**
- **The later opening pages shift into…**
- **This sequence adds… without yet…**
- **The stretch from X to Y keeps introducing…**
- **The scene on/with X is carrying… more than…**

### Revision Priorities
Preferred patterns:
- **Clarify X by page 10.**
- **Make Y more legible as the main line of action.**
- **Reduce or compress Z that delays story direction.**
- **Tighten scenes that are carrying X more than Y.**
- **Preserve X, but make Y the clearer priority.**

---

## 6. Sentence patterns to use sparingly

These are allowed, but they can become repetitive or too polished if overused:

- **This category is working.**
- **The opening is stronger in X than in Y.**
- **What works here is X.**
- **The opening is not unclear so much as layered.**
- **This reads more like a tradeoff than a weakness.**
- **The opening keeps replacing one active problem with the next.**

Use them when they are the cleanest sentence. Do not let them become the default shell.

---

## 7. Acceptable page behavior

Every serious block should identify at least one concrete page behavior.

Acceptable page behavior includes:
- the protagonist arriving under pressure
- scenes returning to the same active problem
- dialogue carrying explanation instead of movement
- a shift from one location/problem to the next
- a scene clarifying context without adding direction
- the opening spreading attention across multiple strands
- a repeated scene function across several early scenes
- the setting appearing through working conditions, place cues, or social pressure
- visible strain, embarrassment, avoidance, pursuit, deflection, bargaining, etc.

If a block cannot point to something happening on the page, it is not ready.

---

## 8. Banned pseudo-specificity

These phrases sound analytical but are still too vague if they stand alone:

- the pages are building context
- the story needs more clarity
- the pacing is uneven
- the opening needs more focus
- the characters need more depth
- the dialogue needs more subtext
- the script needs to be sharper
- the stakes are there
- there is pressure
- something matters
- the motivation works
- the conflict is established
- the line of pursuit is unclear
- the objective is weak
- the script has a unique voice
- the material feels fresh
- the world is immersive
- the setting has texture
- the tone is effective
- the pages know what they are

These phrases may appear only if immediately grounded in page behavior. On their own, they are not acceptable.

---

## 9. Common block failures

### Failure 1: Category label disguised as analysis
Example:
- **The tone is strong.**
- **The world is readable.**
- **The protagonist is compelling.**

Problem:
The sentence names a conclusion, not page behavior.

### Failure 2: Smart-sounding abstraction
Example:
- **The opening gives the reader a powerful sense of identity.**
- **The story direction remains under-realized.**

Problem:
The language sounds elevated, but the reader still cannot tell what the pages are doing.

### Failure 3: Generic praise shell
Example:
- **This category is working.**
- **This part of the script is strong.**

Problem:
These can fit almost any report if not followed immediately by a mechanism.

### Failure 4: Repeating another section’s job
Example:
- Primary Findings re-explaining the whole report
- Revision Priorities turning back into diagnosis
- Strengths section becoming a second category-by-category analysis

Problem:
The section loses hierarchy and the report starts to feel padded.

### Failure 5: Stronger phrase without page follow-through
Example:
- **The opening locks onto the protagonist quickly and stays there.**

Problem:
This is acceptable only if followed by what on the page creates that effect.

## 10. Protagonist-strength phrasing rule

Use **“anchors the opening”** as the default preferred short-form judgment for protagonist-strength blocks.

Only vary away from **“anchors the opening”** when a more concrete page-based phrasing is clearly stronger and explicitly shows:
- how the early pages organize themselves around the protagonist
- or how the protagonist becomes the main point of reader attention in the early pages

Any variation must be followed immediately by concrete page behavior.

Preferred examples:
- **The protagonist anchors the opening because the first appearance is active, specific, and already under pressure.**
- **The early pages organize themselves around the protagonist’s problem.**
- **The opening locks onto the protagonist quickly and stays there through the early scene sequence.**

Disallow:
- **gives the opening a center**
- **has a strong presence**
- **is compelling**
- **works well as a lead**

when those phrases appear without concrete page behavior.

---

## 11. Quality checklist

A block meets the standard only if all of the following are true:

1. It makes one clear judgment.
2. It identifies at least one concrete page behavior.
3. It explains why that behavior matters to the read.
4. It could **not** be pasted unchanged into another report without sounding wrong.
5. It is doing the job of the section it is in.
6. It does not rely on banned pseudo-specificity.
7. It does not sound like generic screenplay coverage.
8. If it uses a signature phrase, the phrase is earned by page behavior.
9. If it is a protagonist-strength block, it uses **anchors the opening** or a clearly stronger page-based equivalent.
10. If it is a page-10 direction block, it correctly distinguishes among:
   - clear and consolidated
   - clear but distributed / still consolidating
   - insufficiently clear

---

## 12. Do examples

1. **The opening has reader pull because each early scene replaces one live problem with the next rather than settling the situation.**
2. **Will anchors the opening because his first appearance is active, specific, and already under pressure.**
3. **The pages make the protagonist’s problem clear, but by page 10 it is still not clear enough what the protagonist is trying to do next.**
4. **The opening is clear but distributed: several strong pressures are in play, but the story line is still consolidating.**
5. **The dialogue is highly differentiated, but several exchanges are carrying explanation more than movement.**
6. **The setting helps the read because the opening shows the world through working conditions and social pressure, not just labels.**
7. **The later opening pages shift into explanatory conversation, which starts to slow present-scene movement.**
8. **The stronger scenes add pressure or direction; the weaker ones repeat context the reader already understands.**
9. **What feels least generic here is the protagonist’s immediate problem and surroundings; what is still less defined is where the story is going first.**
10. **The opening does not lack direction entirely; it is still distributing that direction across several active strands.**

---

## 13. Don’t examples

1. **The tone is strong.**
2. **The world is immersive.**
3. **The protagonist is compelling.**
4. **The pages are building context.**
5. **The script has a unique voice.**
6. **The material feels fresh.**
7. **The pacing needs work.**
8. **The stakes are there.**
9. **The opening gives the story a center.**
10. **The line of pursuit is unclear.**

---

## 14. Final rule

The report should feel like a premium instrument, not a polished generic reader response.

Repeat the diagnostic lens, not the sentence shell.
